Permanence Through Implicit Narratives: Indigenous Cartography in the Valley of Mexico

Summer Undergraduate Research Fellowship — AddRan College of Liberal Arts, 2024

A research paper exploring how Indigenous cartographers in the sixteenth-century Valley of Mexico strategically embedded cultural memory into colonial maps. Through mixed artistic and historical analysis, it demonstrates how Indigenous artists created implicit narratives about identity, economics, and environmental relationships — preserving their self-image and historical record within the official cartographic record of the Spanish empire. A companion analysis of the Pintura de Culhuácan, a 1580 map painted by Pedro de San Agustín for King Philip II's Relaciones Geográficas questionnaire, examines how a single map encoded pre-conquest economic activity, Nahuatl glyphs, and community permanence within an ostensibly European document.

Elderly Infrastructure Site Analysis: North Central Texas

Professional Assignment, 2024

A GIS-based site suitability analysis identifying the four highest-concentration zones of elderly population (>65) in North Central Texas — evaluating each site against a weighted infrastructure index including hospitals, urgent care, fire and police stations, shopping centers, parks, and highway access. Produced as a professional deliverable for a simulated commercial real estate client.

Colonial Justification & the Erasure of Aboriginal Australia: A Reading of Dark Emu

Semester Paper, 2025

An analysis of Bruce Pascoe's Dark Emu: Aboriginal Australia and the Birth of Agriculture and its 2023 documentary follow-up, examining the colonial justification narrative that drove British settlers to erase markers of Aboriginal sophistication — including sedentary settlement and agricultural management — to legitimize their occupation. The paper argues that Pascoe's evidence of intentional crop domestication and landscape modification challenges foundational assumptions of Australian colonial identity and forces a reconsideration of the country's historical self-image.

The Thermidorian Reaction & the Return to Revolution

Research Paper, 2023

An analysis of the Thermidorian Reaction of 1794, arguing that the Committee of Public Safety's concentration of power, its use of political terror, and its transformation into an oppressive institution made its overthrow inevitable. The paper examines how the Thermidorians dismantled the Committee and established France's 1795 constitution, tracing the shift in the relationship between the French people and their government — from revolutionary idealism to a cautious attempt to restore democratic principles without repeating the excesses of the Terror.

Human Response to Disease in European Urban Geography

Comparative Analysis, 2023

A comparative analysis examining how European societies have adapted their urban spaces in response to public health crises across two distinct periods — the Black Death in medieval England (1350–1530) and the Covid-19 pandemic in the EU (2020–2022). Drawing on Lilley's work on post-plague urban planning and MMP et al.'s study of outdoor retail markets, the paper argues that human responses to disease produce lasting transformations in how urban space is produced and used.
